Abstract
This paper introduces a theoretical framework for investigating analytic maps from finite
discrete data, elucidating mathematical machinery underlying the polynomial approximation
withleast-squaresinmultivariatesituations. Ourapproachistoconsiderthepush-forwardon
thespaceoflocallyanalyticfunctionals,insteadofdirectlyhandlingtheanalyticmapitself. We
establish a methodology enabling appropriate finite-dimensional approximation of the push-
forward from finite discrete data, through the theory of the Fourier–Borel transform and the
Fockspace. Moreover,weprovearigorousconvergenceresultwithaconvergencerate. Asan
application, we prove that it is not the least-squares polynomial, but the polynomial obtained
bytruncatingitshigher-degreeterms, thatapproximatesanalyticfunctionsandfurtherallows
forapproximationbeyondthesupportofthedatadistribution. Oneadvantageofourtheoryis
that it enables us to apply linear algebraic operations to the finite-dimensional approximation
of the push-forward. Utilizing this, we prove the convergence of a method for approximating
ananalyticvectorfieldfromfinitedataoftheflowmapofanordinarydifferentialequation.
Keywords— push-forward on locally analytic functionals, Fourier–Borel transform, Fock space, poly-
nomial approximation, least-squares, ordinary differential equation, dynamical system, Perron–Frobenius
operator

There are several articles handling convergence of the least-squares polynomial (for example
[5, 1, 10, 6] and reference therein). They basically impose an additional assumption on the shape
of the sample points (nodes), which is usually called the “mesh” on the approximation domain,
and prove convergence within the support of the data distribution. In the context of the Lagrange
interpolation, several articles [16, 25, 7, 24] show that by suitably designing the nodes, entire
functions can be approximated by Lagrange interpolating polynomials in the sense of the uniform
convergenceonthecompactsets.
In contrast to these articles, Theorem 1.3 imposes minimal assumptions on the distribution of
dataandprovesconvergencethroughtruncatingthehigher-degreetermsoftheleast-squarespoly-
nomialwithasufficientnumberofdata,ratherthanconsideringanappropriatemesh. Furthermore,
it has been proven that the truncated polynomial obtained from the least-squares converges to the
analyticfunctionbeyondthesupportofthedatadistribution. Asindicatedbytheappearanceofthe
term mγm in the convergence rate of (1.6), the truncation of the higher-degree terms of the least-
squares polynomial is essential. We also note that Theorem 1.3 reflects the Runge phenomenon.
